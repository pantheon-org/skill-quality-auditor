# Classical Foundations of the Socratic Method

## Elenchus

The core of Socratic questioning. A dialectic method that tests a hypothesis through
cross-examination. The goal is not to win the argument but to expose contradictions in
the interlocutor's beliefs (aporia).

In this skill: Phase 2 (probe assumptions) and Phase 4 (challenge through hypotheticals)
implement the elenchus — they surface contradictions between stated goals and underlying
assumptions.

## Maieutics

The "midwifery" of ideas. Socrates described himself as a intellectual midwife who helps
others give birth to their own insights rather than implanting knowledge from outside.

In this skill: Phase 5 (synthesize) embodies maieutics. The user arrives at their own
conclusion through guided self-examination.

## Dialectic

The structured exchange of opposing arguments to arrive at truth. Dialectic requires both
parties to engage in good faith, submitting their positions to rational scrutiny.

In this skill: The entire 5-phase protocol is a dialectic process. Questions open
possibilities; answers close them into a refined frame.

## Aporia

The state of puzzlement or perplexity that precedes genuine insight. Aporia is not failure —
it is the precondition for learning. Without it, the interlocutor has no reason to revise
their assumptions.

In this skill: Aporia is the desired state after Phase 2 and Phase 4. If the user is not
at least slightly uncertain about their original framing, the questioning has not yet
reached sufficient depth.
